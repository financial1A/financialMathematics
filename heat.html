Heat Equation

The heat equation arises in the modeling of a number of phenomena and is often used in financial mathematics in the modeling of options. The Black–Scholes option pricing model's differential equation can be transformed into the heat equation allowing relatively easy solutions from a familiar body of mathematics. Many of the extensions to the simple option models do not have closed form solutions and thus must be solved numerically to obtain a modeled option price.


The heat equation is a partial differential equation that describes the distribution of heat, or more generally, how a quantity diffuses through a medium over time. In physics, it's used to study the flow of heat in a solid, but it turns out that the same equation can be applied to finance, specifically in the area of option pricing.

In finance, the heat equation is used to model the behavior of options, which are financial instruments that give the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at a specified price on or before a certain date. The Black-Scholes equation, which is a cornerstone of modern financial theory, is actually a transformation of the heat equation. Myron Scholes and Fischer Black developed this equation in the early 1970s, and it earned them a Nobel Prize in Economics.

The Black-Scholes equation is used to determine the fair price of an option, taking into account factors such as the current price of the underlying asset, the option's strike price, the time until expiration, the risk-free interest rate, and the volatility of the asset. The equation is a partial differential equation, just like the heat equation, and it models how the option's price changes over time as these factors change. Now, let me try to give you a rough idea of how the heat equation and the Black-Scholes equation are related. The heat equation can be written as:

∂u/∂t = α ∂²u/∂x²

Here, u is the temperature at a point x and time t, and α is the thermal diffusivity of the material. The equation says that the rate of change of temperature with respect to time is proportional to the second derivative of the temperature with respect to position. The Black-Scholes equation, on the other hand, can be written as:

∂V/∂t + (1/2)σ²S²∂²V/∂S² + rS∂V/∂S - rV = 0

In this equation, V is the option's price, S is the price of the underlying asset, t is the time, σ is the volatility of the asset, and r is the risk-free interest rate. The equation describes how the option's price changes over time as the underlying asset's price and other factors change.

Now, if you squint your eyes a bit and tilt your head, you might notice that the Black-Scholes equation looks somewhat similar to the heat equation. In fact, with a bit of mathematical wizardry, you can transform the Black-Scholes equation into a form that looks almost exactly like the heat equation. This transformation involves changing variables and applying some scaling factors, but the basic structure of the equation remains the same. So, what does this all mean? Well, it means that the same mathematical tools and techniques that physicists use to study the flow of heat in a solid can also be used to study the behavior of options in the financial markets. It's a beautiful example of how ideas from one field can be applied to another, and it shows that there's a deep connection between the seemingly unrelated worlds of physics and finance. In conclusion, the heat equation plays a crucial role in finance analysis, particularly in option pricing. The Black-Scholes equation, which is derived from the heat equation, has become a fundamental tool for financial analysts and traders. It's a testament to the power of mathematics and the interconnectedness of knowledge that a concept from physics can have such a profound impact on the world of finance.
